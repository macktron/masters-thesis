\section{Comparison with Related Work}\label{sec:disc:comparison}

Relative to classical \gls{elint} fingerprinter pipelines surveyed in \cref{sec:related:classical}, the proposed approach trades hand-engineered feature logic for a learned encoder, at the cost of data hunger, compute, and reduced transparency unless accompanied by diagnostics such as those in \cref{sec:exp:qualitative}.
Unlike the supervised radar classifiers discussed in \cref{sec:related:dl_radar}, which typically assume a fixed global label set for emitters, the training objective here treats emitter identity as a \emph{train-local} symbol used only through within-train comparisons (\cref{sec:method:loss}); operating modes, by contrast, are predicted against the same kind of global catalogue used in those classifiers.

Deep clustering methods in \cref{sec:related:deep_clustering} typically optimise a \emph{single} flat partition without labels, or attach two unrelated supervised heads to the same backbone.
This thesis instead targets two aligned partitions with a nesting interpretation (modes within emitters), with asymmetric supervision: a within-train supervised contrastive signal for the emitter branch and a global classification signal for the mode branch.
The setup is closer in spirit to offline pulse-level grouping work that reasons about geometry and overlap in \gls{esm} data \parencite{bedoire2022offlineclustering} but uses gradient-based representation learning rather than hand-tuned density stages alone.
Where scores fall short of the best published supervised benchmarks, the gap is expected: those models often operate on curated single-emitter tracks with a flat, globally consistent emitter label set, whereas the present setting stresses mixture windows and a label structure that forces emitter inference through clustering rather than through a global classifier.
