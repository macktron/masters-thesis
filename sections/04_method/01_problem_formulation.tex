\section{Problem Formulation}\label{sec:method:formulation}

This chapter studies unsupervised structure recovery from a time-ordered stream of \glspl{pdw} produced by an \gls{esm} or deinterleaving front end, as outlined in \cref{sec:bg:ew_pipeline}.
The goal is twofold: associate each pulse with a physical \emph{emitter instance} and, within that instance, with an \emph{operating mode}, without prior knowledge of how many emitters or modes appear in the data.
Both quantities are latent because labels are scarce in \gls{elint} settings and because novel emitters and modes must be accommodated.

\subsection*{Observed input}

Let a sequence of $N$ pulses be represented by feature vectors
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:pdw_sequence}
  \mat{X} = (\vect{x}_1,\ldots,\vect{x}_N),
  \qquad
  \vect{x}_n \in \R^{d},
\end{equation}
where each $\vect{x}_n$ encodes the measured pulse parameters associated with one \gls{pdw} (carrier frequency, pulse width, timing, spatial cues, and any auxiliary fields).
The sequence may contain irregular sampling, missing pulses, and spurious detections inherited from upstream processing; robustness to such effects is a design constraint rather than part of the formal specification.

\subsection*{Latent emitter and mode assignments}

For each index $n \in \{1,\ldots,N\}$, let $e_n$ denote the emitter identity responsible for pulse $n$ and let $m_n$ denote the operating mode within that emitter.
These variables take values in finite label sets whose cardinalities
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:cardinalities}
  K_{\mathrm{em}} = \lvert \{e_1,\ldots,e_N\} \rvert,
  \qquad
  K_{\mathrm{md}} = \lvert \{m_1,\ldots,m_N\} \rvert
\end{equation}
are \emph{unknown a priori} and need not match the number of physical platforms in the environment (for example, co-located antennas may map to a single inferred emitter index, or a single platform may split across indices when observations are ambiguous).
The pair $(e_n,m_n)$ is not observed at training time; the learning problem is to infer representations from which both clusterings can be recovered reliably.

\subsection*{Desired outputs: dual embeddings and discrete labels}

The parametric model considered in this thesis maps each input sequence to two parallel trajectories in Euclidean embedding spaces,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:dual_embeddings}
  \mat{Z}^{\mathrm{em}} = (\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_1,\ldots,\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_N),
  \quad
  \vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n \in \R^{p},
  \qquad
  \mat{Z}^{\mathrm{md}} = (\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_1,\ldots,\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_N),
  \quad
  \vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n \in \R^{q},
\end{equation}
with dimensions $p$ and $q$ fixed by the architecture.
Discrete emitter labels $\hat{e}_n$ and mode labels $\hat{m}_n$ are obtained \emph{post hoc} by clustering $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n\}_{n=1}^{N}$ and $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n\}_{n=1}^{N}$, or by reading off prototype assignments when the training objective includes cluster parameters.
Thus the immediate trainable targets are the continuous vectors in \cref{eq:method:dual_embeddings}; hard partitions are induced indirectly.

The two trajectories serve different roles.
Emitter-oriented vectors $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n$ are primarily a vehicle for estimating consistent emitter membership; their main deliverable is a per-pulse emitter index suitable for downstream \gls{elint} workflows such as emitter counting and localisation.
Mode-oriented vectors $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n$ additionally aim to summarise mode-dependent waveform behaviour so that operating modes can be inspected, compared, or matched against a library of known modes once such references become available.

Architecturally, both streams are produced from a shared encoder $\Enc$ with task-specific heads (see \cref{sec:method:arch}); in deployment, emitter embeddings may be less critical to retain after discrete $\hat{e}_n$ have been fixed, whereas mode embeddings (or intermediate encoder activations feeding the mode head) are natural inputs to specialised mode classifiers.

\subsection*{Learning objective}

The optimisation problem couples representation learning with clustering-friendly losses so that the geometry of $\mat{Z}^{\mathrm{em}}$ and $\mat{Z}^{\mathrm{md}}$ aligns with emitter and mode structure, respectively.
The concrete loss terms and training protocol are given in \cref{sec:method:loss,sec:method:training}.
