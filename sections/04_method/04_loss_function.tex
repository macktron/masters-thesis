\section{Loss Function}\label{sec:method:loss}

The trainable objective applies the same \emph{supervised contrastive} functional form to both label streams introduced in \cref{sec:method:formulation}, with positive sets chosen so that each term respects its label's scope: positives for the emitter branch are restricted to the current window (because emitter symbols are train-local), whereas positives for the mode branch are drawn from anywhere in the batch (because the mode catalogue is global).
The two terms are summed with a weight on the mode branch and minimised jointly; the post-hoc clustering of the resulting embeddings, as specified in \cref{eq:method:emitter_predictor,eq:method:mode_predictor}, is left for inference time and is not part of the training objective.

\subsection*{Supervised contrastive viewpoint}

Contrastive objectives shape the geometry of an embedding space by encouraging positives (semantically related samples) to lie close together while pushing unrelated negatives apart \parencite{chen2020simclr}.
In the original SimCLR formulation, positives are constructed without supervision from two stochastic augmentations of the same input, and the loss is closely related to a lower bound on mutual information between two views in contrastive predictive coding \parencite{oord2018representation}.
When per-sample labels are available, the same machinery generalises to \emph{supervised contrastive} learning, in which every other sample sharing the anchor's label is treated as a positive and the remaining samples are negatives \parencite{khosla2020supcon}.

The supervised variant is a natural fit for both tasks in \cref{sec:method:formulation} and is used uniformly across the emitter and mode branches; the two branches differ only in the \emph{scope} from which positives and negatives are drawn.
For the emitter branch, every pulse in a pulse train carries an emitter label and the positive set for an anchor is exactly the set of other pulses in the same window with the same emitter symbol; because emitter symbols are unique only within a pulse train, contrasts are restricted to the current window, which matches both the train-local semantics of $e_n$ and the windowing strategy in \cref{sec:method:data}.
For the mode branch, every pulse carries a global mode label that refers to the same operating mode across all pulse trains, so positives and negatives are pooled across the entire mini-batch, making the mode-branch supervision identical to the standard SupCon setting \parencite{khosla2020supcon}.
A large set of negatives is essential in either case: without repulsive pressure on unrelated pairs, encoders can collapse to a constant map and still achieve low training error on a naïve agreement loss \parencite{chen2020simclr}.

Routing both branches through supervised contrastive losses --- rather than attaching a closed-catalogue softmax head to the mode branch --- keeps the inference path through clustering open and is the reason both branches can in principle handle previously unseen labels: emitters by virtue of the train-local re-indexing, and modes by virtue of the open-set clustering described in \cref{sec:method:formulation,sec:method:arch}.

\subsection*{Supervised contrastive building block}

Let $\phi(\vect{z}) = \vect{z}/\lVert \vect{z} \rVert_2$ denote $\ell_2$ normalisation and let $\tau > 0$ denote the contrastive temperature.
Write the scaled cosine similarity as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:sim_tau}
  s_\tau(\vect{a},\vect{b}) = \frac{1}{\tau}\,\phi(\vect{a})^\top \phi(\vect{b}).
\end{equation}
The temperature $\tau$ rescales inner products after normalisation: smaller $\tau$ sharpens the softmax in \cref{eq:method:supcon}, so the loss concentrates on the hardest negatives and gradients emphasise fine separation on the sphere, whereas larger $\tau$ yields a softer distribution closer to uniform and can stabilise optimisation when embeddings are poorly separated early in training \parencite{chen2020simclr}.
All contrastive terms in this thesis share a fixed $\tau = 0.2$, in line with values commonly used for normalised contrastive features \parencite{chen2020simclr,khosla2020supcon}.

Given a finite anchor set $\mathcal{I}$ with embeddings $\{\vect{z}_i\}_{i \in \mathcal{I}}$ and, for each anchor $i \in \mathcal{I}$, a positive set $\mathcal{P}(i) \subseteq \mathcal{I} \setminus \{i\}$ defined by label coincidence, the supervised contrastive contribution averages a per-positive InfoNCE / NT-Xent term over $\mathcal{P}(i)$ \parencite{khosla2020supcon}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:supcon}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(\mathcal{I},\{\mathcal{P}(i)\}_{i \in \mathcal{I}}\bigr)
  =
  -\frac{1}{\lvert \mathcal{I}^{+} \rvert}
  \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}^{+}}
  \frac{1}{\lvert \mathcal{P}(i) \rvert}
  \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}(i)}
  \log
  \frac{\exp\bigl(s_\tau(\vect{z}_i,\vect{z}_p)\bigr)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{\substack{j \in \mathcal{I} \\ j \neq i}} \exp\bigl(s_\tau(\vect{z}_i,\vect{z}_j)\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{I}^{+} = \{i \in \mathcal{I} : \mathcal{P}(i) \neq \emptyset\}$ collects anchors with at least one positive available, and the convention $\Loss_{\mathrm{SC}} = 0$ is used when $\mathcal{I}^{+}$ is empty.
The denominator collects every candidate in $\mathcal{I}$ except the anchor itself, so unrelated pulses act as negatives and collapse is discouraged \parencite{chen2020simclr}.

\subsection*{Emitter-oriented term}

The emitter branch targets a representation in which pulses from the same train-local emitter are close and pulses from different emitters are separated, which is the geometric content expected of a deinterleaving embedding \parencite{gunn2025deinterleaving}.
For a window of $L$ pulses with emitter labels $(e_1,\ldots,e_L)$ and emitter embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n\}_{n=1}^{L}$ obtained from \cref{eq:method:proj_heads}, the within-window positive set for anchor position $n$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:emitter_positives}
  \mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_n = \{\, n' \in \{1,\ldots,L\}\setminus\{n\} : e_{n'} = e_n \,\}.
\end{equation}
The per-window emitter loss is the supervised contrastive contribution \cref{eq:method:supcon} on the index set $\{1,\ldots,L\}$ with these positive sets, and the batch loss is the mean over the $B$ windows in a mini-batch:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_emitter}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{em}}
  =
  \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(\{1,\ldots,L\},\, \{\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_{b,n}\}_{n=1}^{L}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where the subscript $b$ in $\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_{b,n}$ indicates that positives are determined by the emitter labels of window $b$.
Restricting both anchors and negatives to the current window enforces the train-local semantics of the emitter symbol from \cref{sec:method:formulation}: pulses from different pulse trains are never compared, so the loss never attaches meaning to coincidences between emitter integers across windows.
A pulse whose emitter is unique inside its window (so $\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_n = \emptyset$) contributes only as a negative for other anchors and is excluded from the per-window average, following the convention in \cref{eq:method:supcon}.

\subsection*{Mode-oriented term}

Operating modes carry meaning across pulse trains, and every pulse in the training data is annotated with a global mode label $m_n \in \{1,\ldots,M\}$.
The mode branch uses the same supervised contrastive functional form as the emitter branch but with positives drawn across the whole mini-batch.
Concatenate the per-window pulse sequences of a mini-batch into a global index set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:mode_index_set}
  \mathcal{I}^{\mathrm{md}} = \{\,(b,n) : b \in \{1,\ldots,B\},\, n \in \{1,\ldots,L\}\,\},
\end{equation}
together with the corresponding embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n}\}_{(b,n) \in \mathcal{I}^{\mathrm{md}}}$ and labels $\{m_{b,n}\}_{(b,n) \in \mathcal{I}^{\mathrm{md}}}$.
The positive set for an anchor $(b,n)$ is then the set of all other pulses in the batch with the same global mode label,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:mode_positives}
  \mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n} = \{\, (b',n') \in \mathcal{I}^{\mathrm{md}} \setminus \{(b,n)\} : m_{b',n'} = m_{b,n} \,\},
\end{equation}
and the mode loss is the supervised contrastive contribution \cref{eq:method:supcon} on the batch-wide index set:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_mode}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{md}}
  =
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(\mathcal{I}^{\mathrm{md}},\, \{\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n}\}_{(b,n) \in \mathcal{I}^{\mathrm{md}}}\bigr).
\end{equation}
Because the catalogue $\{1,\ldots,M\}$ is shared across windows, batch-wide pooling pulls together embeddings whose ground-truth modes coincide regardless of which physical emitter or which train produced them, and pushes apart embeddings of pulses belonging to different modes.
Crucially, the loss never references the cardinality $M$ explicitly: it only consumes pairwise label coincidences.
Consequently, embeddings of modes that are held out during training receive no direct gradient signal but still inherit the geometry induced by their neighbours in $\R^{q}$, which is the geometric prerequisite for the open-set mode clustering at inference described in \cref{sec:method:formulation,sec:method:arch}.

\subsection*{Combined objective and use of the projection heads}

The overall training loss is the weighted sum
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_total}
  \Loss = \Loss_{\mathrm{em}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{md}}\,\Loss_{\mathrm{md}},
  \qquad
  \lambda_{\mathrm{md}} > 0,
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\mathrm{md}}$ treated as a hyperparameter and reported in \cref{ch:experiments}.

For emitter inference on a previously unseen pulse train, the deliverable is the discrete per-pulse assignment $\hat{e}_n$ in \cref{eq:method:emitter_predictor}, obtained by clustering the emitter embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n\}$ within each test window with the post-hoc clustering algorithm of \cref{sec:method:arch}.
For mode inference, the analogous discrete assignment $\hat{m}_n$ in \cref{eq:method:mode_predictor} is obtained by applying the same per-window clustering to the mode embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n\}$; the two branches therefore share their inference pipeline and differ only in the embeddings they consume and in the scope of the supervision that shaped them.
The clustering for the mode branch imposes no prior on the number of clusters $\hat{M}$, so $\hat{M}$ may exceed the size $M$ of the training-time catalogue when modulation types absent from training are encountered at inference; recovering catalogue-level mode identities from these per-window cluster indices is left as an evaluation-time alignment problem handled by the permutation-invariant metrics of \cref{sec:method:metrics}.
The continuous embeddings $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n$ and $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n$ remain useful for inspection and for downstream tasks that benefit from a geometric rather than purely categorical representation, but once $\hat{e}_n$ and $\hat{m}_n$ have been fixed, storing every embedding is optional for operational consumers of the discrete output.
