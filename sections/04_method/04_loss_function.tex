\section{Loss Function}\label{sec:method:loss}

The trainable objective uses both label streams introduced in \cref{sec:method:formulation}: a within-window \emph{supervised contrastive} loss that uses the train-local emitter labels $\{e_n\}$ to shape $\mat{Z}^{\mathrm{em}}$, and a per-pulse cross-entropy loss that uses the global mode labels $\{m_n\}$ to shape $\mat{Z}^{\mathrm{md}}$ through the classification head from \cref{eq:method:mode_predictor}.
The two terms are summed with a weight on the mode branch and minimised jointly.

\subsection*{Supervised contrastive viewpoint}

Contrastive objectives shape the geometry of an embedding space by encouraging positives (semantically related samples) to lie close together while pushing unrelated negatives apart \parencite{chen2020simclr}.
In the original SimCLR formulation, positives are constructed without supervision from two stochastic augmentations of the same input, and the loss is closely related to a lower bound on mutual information between two views in contrastive predictive coding \parencite{oord2018representation}.
When per-sample labels are available, the same machinery generalises to \emph{supervised contrastive} learning, in which every other sample sharing the anchor's label is treated as a positive and the remaining samples are negatives \parencite{khosla2020supcon}.

The supervised variant is a natural fit for the emitter task in \cref{sec:method:formulation}: within each pulse-train window, every pulse carries an emitter label and the positive set for an anchor is exactly the set of other pulses with the same emitter symbol.
Because emitter symbols are unique only within a pulse train, contrasts are restricted to the current window, which matches both the train-local semantics of $e_n$ and the windowing strategy in \cref{sec:method:data}.
A large set of negatives drawn from the same window is essential in either viewpoint: without repulsive pressure on unrelated pairs, encoders can collapse to a constant map and still achieve low training error on a naïve agreement loss \parencite{chen2020simclr}.

\subsection*{Supervised contrastive building block}

Let $\phi(\vect{z}) = \vect{z}/\lVert \vect{z} \rVert_2$ denote $\ell_2$ normalisation and let $\tau > 0$ denote the contrastive temperature.
Write the scaled cosine similarity as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:sim_tau}
  s_\tau(\vect{a},\vect{b}) = \frac{1}{\tau}\,\phi(\vect{a})^\top \phi(\vect{b}).
\end{equation}
The temperature $\tau$ rescales inner products after normalisation: smaller $\tau$ sharpens the softmax in \cref{eq:method:supcon}, so the loss concentrates on the hardest negatives and gradients emphasise fine separation on the sphere, whereas larger $\tau$ yields a softer distribution closer to uniform and can stabilise optimisation when embeddings are poorly separated early in training \parencite{chen2020simclr}.
All contrastive terms in this thesis share a fixed $\tau = 0.2$, in line with values commonly used for normalised contrastive features \parencite{chen2020simclr,khosla2020supcon}.

Given a finite anchor set $\mathcal{I}$ with embeddings $\{\vect{z}_i\}_{i \in \mathcal{I}}$ and, for each anchor $i \in \mathcal{I}$, a positive set $\mathcal{P}(i) \subseteq \mathcal{I} \setminus \{i\}$ defined by label coincidence, the supervised contrastive contribution averages a per-positive InfoNCE / NT-Xent term over $\mathcal{P}(i)$ \parencite{khosla2020supcon}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:supcon}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(\mathcal{I},\{\mathcal{P}(i)\}_{i \in \mathcal{I}}\bigr)
  =
  -\frac{1}{\lvert \mathcal{I}^{+} \rvert}
  \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}^{+}}
  \frac{1}{\lvert \mathcal{P}(i) \rvert}
  \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}(i)}
  \log
  \frac{\exp\bigl(s_\tau(\vect{z}_i,\vect{z}_p)\bigr)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{\substack{j \in \mathcal{I} \\ j \neq i}} \exp\bigl(s_\tau(\vect{z}_i,\vect{z}_j)\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{I}^{+} = \{i \in \mathcal{I} : \mathcal{P}(i) \neq \emptyset\}$ collects anchors with at least one positive available, and the convention $\Loss_{\mathrm{SC}} = 0$ is used when $\mathcal{I}^{+}$ is empty.
The denominator collects every candidate in $\mathcal{I}$ except the anchor itself, so unrelated pulses act as negatives and collapse is discouraged \parencite{chen2020simclr}.

\subsection*{Emitter-oriented term}

The emitter branch targets a representation in which pulses from the same train-local emitter are close and pulses from different emitters are separated, which is the geometric content expected of a deinterleaving embedding \parencite{gunn2025deinterleaving}.
For a window of $L$ pulses with emitter labels $(e_1,\ldots,e_L)$ and emitter embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n\}_{n=1}^{L}$ obtained from \cref{eq:method:proj_heads}, the within-window positive set for anchor position $n$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:emitter_positives}
  \mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_n = \{\, n' \in \{1,\ldots,L\}\setminus\{n\} : e_{n'} = e_n \,\}.
\end{equation}
The per-window emitter loss is the supervised contrastive contribution \cref{eq:method:supcon} on the index set $\{1,\ldots,L\}$ with these positive sets, and the batch loss is the mean over the $B$ windows in a mini-batch:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_emitter}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{em}}
  =
  \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(\{1,\ldots,L\},\, \{\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_{b,n}\}_{n=1}^{L}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where the subscript $b$ in $\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_{b,n}$ indicates that positives are determined by the emitter labels of window $b$.
Restricting both anchors and negatives to the current window enforces the train-local semantics of the emitter symbol from \cref{sec:method:formulation}: pulses from different pulse trains are never compared, so the loss never attaches meaning to coincidences between emitter integers across windows.
A pulse whose emitter is unique inside its window (so $\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{em}}_n = \emptyset$) contributes only as a negative for other anchors and is excluded from the per-window average, following the convention in \cref{eq:method:supcon}.

\subsection*{Mode-oriented term}

Operating modes carry meaning across pulse trains, and every pulse in the training data is annotated with a global mode label $m_n \in \{1,\ldots,M\}$.
A linear classification head $f \colon \R^{q} \to \R^{M}$ on top of the mode embedding produces per-class logits, and the mode branch is trained with the standard per-pulse cross-entropy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_mode}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{md}}
  =
  -\frac{1}{B\,L}
  \sum_{b=1}^{B}\sum_{n=1}^{L}
  \log
  \frac{\exp\bigl(f_{m_{b,n}}\!\bigl(\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n}\bigr)\bigr)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{\ell=1}^{M}\exp\bigl(f_{\ell}\!\bigl(\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n}\bigr)\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $m_{b,n}$ denotes the ground-truth mode label of pulse $n$ in window $b$ and $f$ is the head used at inference in \cref{eq:method:mode_predictor}.
Because the catalogue $\{1,\ldots,M\}$ is shared across windows, this term applies the same supervision identically to every window, and gradients on $f$ and on $\Enc$ pull together mode embeddings whose ground-truth modes coincide regardless of which physical emitter or which train produced them.
Auxiliary contrastive structure on $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n$ (for example, a SupCon term that uses mode labels as positives \parencite{khosla2020supcon}) is a natural extension when classifier confidence alone is insufficient to organise mode geometry; whether such a term is active is part of the optimisation configuration recorded in \cref{ch:experiments}.

\subsection*{Combined objective and use of the projection heads}

The overall training loss is the weighted sum
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_total}
  \Loss = \Loss_{\mathrm{em}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{md}}\,\Loss_{\mathrm{md}},
  \qquad
  \lambda_{\mathrm{md}} > 0,
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\mathrm{md}}$ treated as a hyperparameter and reported in \cref{ch:experiments}.

For emitter inference on a previously unseen pulse train, the deliverable is the discrete per-pulse assignment $\hat{e}_n$ in \cref{eq:method:emitter_predictor}, obtained by clustering the emitter embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n\}$ within each test window with the post-hoc clustering algorithm of \cref{sec:method:arch}; once those labels are fixed, storing every $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n$ is optional because the continuous representation is not required for the operational tasks that consume hard emitter tracks.
For mode inference, the per-pulse prediction $\hat{m}_n$ is read directly from the classification head as in \cref{eq:method:mode_predictor}, while the mode embeddings $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n$ remain useful for visualisation, nearest-neighbour mode analysis, and any future matching against external waveform libraries.
