\section{Loss Function}\label{sec:method:loss}

The training objective applies the same \emph{supervised contrastive} functional form to both label streams introduced in \cref{sec:method:formulation}, with positive sets chosen so that each term respects its label's scope: positives for the emitter branch are restricted to the current window (because emitter symbols are train-local), whereas positives for the mode branch are drawn from anywhere in the batch (because the mode catalogue is global).
The two terms are summed with a weight on the mode branch and minimised jointly; the post-hoc clustering of the resulting embeddings, as specified in \cref{eq:method:emitter_predictor,eq:method:mode_predictor}, is left for inference time and is not part of the training objective.

\subsection*{Supervised contrastive viewpoint}

Contrastive objectives shape the geometry of an embedding space by encouraging positives (semantically related samples) to lie close together while pushing unrelated negatives apart \parencite{chen2020simclr}.
In the self-supervised setting of SimCLR \parencite{chen2020simclr} and contrastive predictive coding \parencite{oord2018representation}, positives are constructed without supervision from two stochastic augmentations of the same input and the resulting InfoNCE / NT-Xent objective can be interpreted as a lower bound on the mutual information between the two views \parencite{oord2018representation}.
When per-sample labels are available, the same machinery generalises to \emph{supervised contrastive} learning \parencite{khosla2020supcon}, in which the single augmentation-based positive is replaced by the full set of other samples sharing the anchor's label and the remaining samples in the batch act as negatives; the loss in \cref{eq:method:supcon} below is the $\Loss^{\mathrm{sup}}_{\mathrm{out}}$ formulation recommended by \textcite{khosla2020supcon} for its more favourable gradient structure.

The supervised variant is a natural fit for both tasks in \cref{sec:method:formulation} and is used uniformly across the emitter and mode branches; the two differ only in the \emph{scope} from which positives and negatives are drawn.
For the emitter branch, every pulse in a window carries an emitter label and the positives of an anchor are the other pulses in the same window with the same emitter symbol; because emitter symbols are unique only within a pulse train, contrasts are restricted to the current window, which matches both the train-local semantics of $e_n$ and the windowing strategy in \cref{sec:method:data}.
For the mode branch, every pulse carries a global mode label that refers to the same operating mode across all pulse trains, so positives and negatives are pooled across the entire mini-batch, making the mode-branch supervision identical to the standard SupCon setting \parencite{khosla2020supcon}.
A large set of negatives is essential in either case: without repulsive pressure on unrelated pairs, encoders can collapse to a constant map and still achieve low training error on a naïve agreement loss \parencite{chen2020simclr}.

Routing both branches through supervised contrastive losses --- rather than attaching a closed-catalogue softmax head to the mode branch --- keeps the inference path through clustering open and is the reason both branches can in principle handle previously unseen labels: emitters by virtue of the train-local re-indexing, and modes by virtue of the open-set clustering described in \cref{sec:method:formulation,sec:method:arch}.

\subsection*{Supervised contrastive building block}

All embeddings produced by the projection heads of \cref{eq:method:proj_heads} are $\ell_2$-normalised before the loss is evaluated, so that the inner product equals the cosine similarity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:sim}
  \mathrm{sim}(\vect{z}_i,\vect{z}_j) = \frac{\vect{z}_i^\top \vect{z}_j}{\lVert \vect{z}_i \rVert_2\,\lVert \vect{z}_j \rVert_2} \in [-1,1].
\end{equation}
A scalar temperature $\tau > 0$ rescales these similarities inside the softmax of the loss: smaller $\tau$ sharpens the distribution so the loss concentrates on the hardest negatives, whereas larger $\tau$ yields a softer distribution and stabilises optimisation when embeddings are poorly separated early in training \parencite{chen2020simclr}.
All contrastive terms in this thesis share a fixed $\tau = 0.2$, in line with values commonly used for normalised contrastive features \parencite{chen2020simclr,khosla2020supcon}.

Given a finite anchor set $I$ with embeddings $\{\vect{z}_i\}_{i \in I}$, write $A(i) = I \setminus \{i\}$ for the candidate set of anchor $i$ and let $P(i) \subseteq A(i)$ be its positive set, defined by label coincidence.
The supervised contrastive contribution is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:supcon}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(I,\{P(i)\}_{i \in I}\bigr)
  =
  \sum_{i \in I^{+}}
  \frac{-1}{\lvert P(i) \rvert}
  \sum_{p \in P(i)}
  \log
  \frac{\exp\!\bigl(\mathrm{sim}(\vect{z}_i,\vect{z}_p)/\tau\bigr)}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{a \in A(i)} \exp\!\bigl(\mathrm{sim}(\vect{z}_i,\vect{z}_a)/\tau\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $I^{+} = \{\,i \in I : P(i) \neq \emptyset\,\}$ collects the anchors with at least one positive available, and $\Loss_{\mathrm{SC}} = 0$ when $I^{+}$ is empty.
The denominator runs over every candidate in $A(i)$, so unrelated pulses act as negatives and collapse to a constant map is discouraged \parencite{chen2020simclr}; anchors with $P(i) = \emptyset$ contribute only as negatives for other anchors.

\subsection*{Emitter-oriented term}

The emitter branch targets a representation in which pulses from the same train-local emitter are close and pulses from different emitters are separated, which is the geometric content expected of a deinterleaving embedding \parencite{gunn2025deinterleaving}.
For a window of $L$ pulses with emitter labels $(e_1,\ldots,e_L)$ and emitter embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n\}_{n=1}^{L}$ from \cref{eq:method:proj_heads}, the within-window anchor set is $I^{\mathrm{em}}_b = \{1,\ldots,L\}$ and the positive set of anchor $n$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:emitter_positives}
  P^{\mathrm{em}}_{b,n} = \{\, n' \in I^{\mathrm{em}}_b \setminus \{n\} : e_{n'} = e_n \,\},
\end{equation}
where the subscript $b$ indicates that the labels are read from window $b$.
The per-window emitter loss is the supervised contrastive contribution \cref{eq:method:supcon} evaluated on $I^{\mathrm{em}}_b$ with these positive sets, and the batch loss is the mean over the $B$ windows in a mini-batch:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_emitter}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{em}}
  =
  \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(I^{\mathrm{em}}_b,\, \{P^{\mathrm{em}}_{b,n}\}_{n \in I^{\mathrm{em}}_b}\bigr).
\end{equation}
Restricting both anchors and negatives to the current window enforces the train-local semantics of the emitter symbol from \cref{sec:method:formulation}: pulses from different pulse trains are never compared, so the loss never attaches meaning to coincidences between emitter integers across windows.
A pulse whose emitter is unique inside its window has $P^{\mathrm{em}}_{b,n} = \emptyset$ and contributes only as a negative for other anchors, following the convention in \cref{eq:method:supcon}.

\subsection*{Mode-oriented term}

Operating modes carry meaning across pulse trains, and every pulse in the training data is annotated with a global mode label $m_n \in \{1,\ldots,M\}$.
The mode branch uses the same functional form as the emitter branch, but with the anchor set pooled across the whole mini-batch.
Concatenating the per-window pulse sequences yields a global anchor set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:mode_index_set}
  I^{\mathrm{md}} = \{\,(b,n) : b \in \{1,\ldots,B\},\, n \in \{1,\ldots,L\}\,\},
\end{equation}
together with the corresponding embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n}\}_{(b,n) \in I^{\mathrm{md}}}$ and labels $\{m_{b,n}\}_{(b,n) \in I^{\mathrm{md}}}$.
The positive set of an anchor $(b,n)$ is the set of all other pulses in the batch with the same global mode label,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:mode_positives}
  P^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n} = \{\, (b',n') \in I^{\mathrm{md}} \setminus \{(b,n)\} : m_{b',n'} = m_{b,n} \,\},
\end{equation}
and the mode loss is the supervised contrastive contribution \cref{eq:method:supcon} on this batch-wide anchor set:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_mode}
  \Loss_{\mathrm{md}}
  =
  \Loss_{\mathrm{SC}}\bigl(I^{\mathrm{md}},\, \{P^{\mathrm{md}}_{b,n}\}_{(b,n) \in I^{\mathrm{md}}}\bigr).
\end{equation}
Because the catalogue $\{1,\ldots,M\}$ is shared across windows, batch-wide pooling pulls together embeddings whose ground-truth modes coincide regardless of which physical emitter or which train produced them, and pushes apart embeddings of pulses from different modes.
Crucially, the loss never references the cardinality $M$ explicitly; it only consumes pairwise label coincidences.
Embeddings of modes that are held out during training therefore receive no direct gradient signal but still inherit the geometry induced by their neighbours in $\R^{q}$, which is the geometric prerequisite for the open-set mode clustering at inference described in \cref{sec:method:formulation,sec:method:arch}.

\subsection*{Combined objective and use of the projection heads}

The overall training loss is the weighted sum
\begin{equation}\label{eq:method:loss_total}
  \Loss = \Loss_{\mathrm{em}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{md}}\,\Loss_{\mathrm{md}},
  \qquad
  \lambda_{\mathrm{md}} > 0,
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\mathrm{md}}$ treated as a hyperparameter and reported in \cref{ch:experiments}.

For emitter inference on a previously unseen pulse train, the deliverable is the discrete per-pulse assignment $\hat{e}_n$ in \cref{eq:method:emitter_predictor}, obtained by clustering the emitter embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n\}$ within each test window with the post-hoc clustering algorithm of \cref{sec:method:arch}.
For mode inference, the analogous discrete assignment $\hat{m}_n$ in \cref{eq:method:mode_predictor} is obtained by applying the same per-window clustering to the mode embeddings $\{\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n\}$; the two branches therefore share their inference pipeline and differ only in the embeddings they consume and in the scope of the supervision that shaped them.
The clustering for the mode branch imposes no prior on the number of clusters $\hat{M}$, so $\hat{M}$ may exceed the size $M$ of the training-time catalogue when modulation types absent from training are encountered at inference; recovering catalogue-level mode identities from these per-window cluster indices is left as an evaluation-time alignment problem handled by the permutation-invariant metrics of \cref{sec:method:metrics}.
The continuous embeddings $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{em}}_n$ and $\vect{z}^{\mathrm{md}}_n$ remain useful for inspection and for downstream tasks that benefit from a geometric rather than purely categorical representation, but once $\hat{e}_n$ and $\hat{m}_n$ have been fixed, storing every embedding is optional for operational consumers of the discrete output.
