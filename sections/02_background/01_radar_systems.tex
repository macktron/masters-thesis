\section{Radar Systems and Passive Reception}\label{sec:bg:radar}

Electromagnetic waves carry energy from a transmitter to one or more receivers.
The same physical mechanism supports both communications and remote sensing: a receiver can infer information from the field strength, timing, and structure of an arriving wave.
\Gls{radar} exploits this idea for echolocation at radio frequencies by comparing transmitted energy to echoes scattered from objects of interest \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.

In a typical pulsed system, a collocated or nearby transmitter and receiver emit short bursts and listen during the intervals between pulses.
Echo delay maps to range under the speed-of-light propagation model, while angular information comes from antenna directivity or multi-channel processing.
Successive pulses are repeated with a nominal \gls{pri} (equivalently, a \gls{prf}), which sets the unambiguous range window and the illumination duty cycle.
On receive, matched filtering and integration improve the effective \gls{snr} before detection and parameter estimation \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.

For a monostatic configuration with common transmit and receive gain $G$, wavelength $\lambda$, target radar cross section $\sigma$, and slant range $R$, the received power obeys the familiar scaling
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:radar}
P_r = \frac{P_t G^2 \lambda^2 \sigma}{(4\pi)^3 R^4},
\end{equation}
where $P_t$ is the peak transmit power during the pulse \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
\cref{eq:bg:radar} shows why range and antenna gain dominate link budgets: $P_r$ falls approximately as $R^{-4}$, so small changes in geometry or losses can produce large swings in echo strength.
In practice, designers also account for atmospheric attenuation, system losses, and processing gains; the simplified form is nevertheless the standard reference for how ``what the radar transmits'' couples to ``what comes back.''

Passive \gls{esm} and \gls{elint} systems observe the same emissions from the sidelobe or mainlobe of a third-party radar, but without control of $P_t$, $G$, or the waveform timing on transmit.
The analyst therefore works from one-way propagation, often with incomplete geometry, and must infer emitter behavior from measurable pulse parameters rather than from a known matched filter reference.
Those per-pulse measurements and their downstream representations are the usual interface to learning-based methods; \cref{sec:bg:pdw} summarizes the corresponding feature view.
