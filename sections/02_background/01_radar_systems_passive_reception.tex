\section{Radar Systems and Passive Reception}\label{sec:bg:radar}

\subsection{Propagation and radiation}

A time-varying current in a conductor sets up propagating electric and magnetic fields; together they carry power and phase information away from the source. An antenna shapes how that power leaves (or enters) the structure, but the underlying mechanism is the same: accelerated charges radiate \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.

\subsection{Active pulsed radar}

\Gls{radar} uses controlled radio-frequency illumination and listens for scattered returns. A transmitter emits short pulses; a receiver records echoes. If the one-way delay is $\tau/2$, the round-trip delay is $\tau$ and the monostatic range estimate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:range}
R = \frac{c\tau}{2},
\end{equation}
with $c$ the speed of light in the propagation medium \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.

Pulse timing repeats with \gls{pri} or \gls{prf}. The largest range for which an echo can be assigned to the pulse that produced it without ambiguity satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:runamb}
R_{\mathrm{u}} = \frac{c}{2\,\mathrm{PRF}} = \frac{c\,\mathrm{PRI}}{2},
\end{equation}
since a late echo from pulse $k$ can be mistaken for an early echo from pulse $k+1$ if $R > R_{\mathrm{u}}$. Coherent processing (matched filtering, integration) raises echo energy relative to noise and interference before detection; that gain is summarized by the \gls{snr} at the detector \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.

\subsection{Received power}

For monostatic geometry (collocated transmit and receive), the peak received power from a resolved scatterer is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:radar}
P_r = \frac{P_t G^2 \lambda^2 \sigma}{(4\pi)^3 R^4},
\end{equation}
where $P_t$ is transmit pulse power, $G$ is antenna gain on transmit and receive (same aperture), $\lambda$ is wavelength, $\sigma$ is radar cross section, and $R$ is slant range \parencite{skolnik2008radar}. The factor $R^{-4}$ is two-way spherical spreading plus inverse-square receive.

\subsection{Passive reception}

\Gls{esm} and \gls{elint} receivers do not transmit. They observe one-way emissions, often via sidelobes, without knowing $P_t$, $G$, or internal timing laws. Inference uses measured pulse parameters (frequency, arrival time, width, angle, amplitude) instead of a cooperative echo model. That measurement-centric view matches the \gls{pdw} interface formalized in \cref{sec:bg:pdw}.

\subsection{Pulse descriptor words}\label{sec:bg:pdw}

After detection and parameter estimation in the pipeline of \cref{sec:bg:elint_pipeline}, each accepted pulse is summarized by a \emph{\gls{pdw}}: a fixed-layout record of measured pulse parameters that discards intermediate waveforms while retaining the quantities \gls{elint} algorithms consume \parencite{wiley2006elint}.
Indexing pulses in time order by $n \in \{1,2,\ldots\}$, write the $n$th descriptor as a real vector
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bg:pdw_vector}
  \vect{x}_n
  =
  \bigl(
    f_n,\,
    \mathrm{PW}_n,\,
    t_n,\,
    A_n,\,
    \theta_n,\,
    \ldots
  \bigr)^{\top}
  \in \R^{d},
\end{equation}
where $f_n$ is the measured carrier frequency, $\mathrm{PW}_n$ the pulse width, $t_n$ the \gls{toa} of a chosen reference point on the pulse (often the leading edge), $A_n$ a scalar amplitude or energy proxy, and $\theta_n$ the \gls{aoa} when direction finding is available.
The ellipsis collects any additional fields the sensor supplies (sub-band identifiers, polarisation, intra-pulse modulation labels, and so on); the dimension $d$ is therefore platform-specific but fixed for a given export format once the column order is fixed.
\cref{fig:bg:pdw} illustrates the core scalar fields in a minimal five-dimensional template aligned with \cref{eq:bg:pdw_vector}.

When pulses are already assigned to a single coherent pulse train, successive \glspl{toa} define inter-pulse intervals
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bg:pdw_pri}
  \tau_n = t_n - t_{n-1}, \qquad n \ge 2,
\end{equation}
from which a nominal \gls{pri} is read directly and a \gls{prf} follows as $\mathrm{PRF}_n = 1/\tau_n$ whenever $\tau_n$ is well defined and the train is approximately periodic.
Staggered or jittered \gls{pri} programs replace a single $\tau_n$ with structured sequences of intervals; in that setting either $\tau_n$ is stored explicitly in the \gls{pdw} stream or stagger parameters appear as additional columns, but the abstract role of \cref{eq:bg:pdw_vector} is unchanged: each index $n$ labels one pulse vector in $\R^{d}$.

Measured descriptors are noisy projections of latent physical state.
A convenient bookkeeping form is $\vect{x}_n = \vect{g}(\vect{s}_n) + \vect{\epsilon}_n$, where $\vect{s}_n$ collects true frequency, timing, and antenna coupling, $\vect{g}$ is the deterministic part of the sensor forward model, and $\vect{\epsilon}_n$ aggregates thermal noise, quantisation, and parameter-estimation error.
Weak pulses yield large effective noise or missing fields; false detections insert spurious samples with no true $\vect{s}_n$.
\emph{Deinterleaving} (\cref{sec:bg:elint_pipeline}) attempts to partition the detection list into trains so that, within each train, \cref{eq:bg:pdw_pri} is interpretable; residual mixture, missed pulses, and extra pulses then appear as structured outliers in the sequence $(\vect{x}_n)$ rather than as isolated scalar errors.

\subsection{Radar emitters and operating modes}\label{sec:bg:emitters}

In passive \gls{esm} and \gls{elint}, a \emph{radar emitter} denotes a physical transmitting source whose emissions can be tied to a train of pulses in time and frequency \parencite{wiley2006elint}, though this coherence has become increasingly complex to discern in modern, modulated radar systems.

An \emph{operating mode}, by contrast, describes the particular way in which a radar system chooses to transmit its signals at a given time.
This includes a set of instructions or parameters that control the radar's outgoing pulses: for example, which frequency the radar uses, whether it changes frequencies over time (hopping law), how long each pulse lasts (pulse width), how often pulses are transmitted (repetition structure), how the radar beam is moved or scanned, and any programmed rules for changing these settings from one pulse to the next \parencite{skolnik2008radar,wiley2006elint}.
While an emitter refers to the underlying physical equipment and its stable identity, a mode is about the operational settings and purposes carried out at a given moment.
The same radar hardware can switch between different modes as its tasks or mission requirements change.

Illustrative mode families include wide-area search, acquisition and track, fire-control illumination, weather sensing, and terminal guidance support \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
Each family implies characteristic constraints on \gls{pri}/\gls{prf}, dwell time, frequency agility, and antenna motion: search modes favor broad coverage and may use staggered or agile repetition to balance range and velocity ambiguity, while dedicated tracking or illuminator modes often concentrate energy in fewer directions with higher update rates on a target of interest.
Navigation radars on ships and aircraft emphasize short-range obstacle and surface mapping; their emissions tend to repeat with comparatively regular timing tied to a fixed sector or rotating beam pattern, whereas long-range surveillance and fire-control systems interleave search and track waveforms and may switch \gls{rf} bands or burst structures when countering interference \parencite{skolnik2008radar,wiley2006elint}.
The resulting diversity matters for downstream analysis because operators must interpret not only ``which radar,'' but also ``what it is doing now,'' and because the same measured \gls{pdw} parameters can arise from different combinations of hardware and scheduling (\cref{sec:bg:pdw}).

The relationship between emitters and modes is many-to-many.
A single emitter implements many modes over a mission timeline, so a time-ordered pulse stream is a mixture of mode segments whose boundaries need not align with changes of physical source.
Conversely, different emitters can realize the same functional mode with different parameter sets---for example, two fire-control radars may both run a track waveform but with distinct nominal \glspl{prf} and frequency plans.
That structure motivates joint reasoning about physical identity and instantaneous configuration: clustering only over pulses loses scheduling context, whereas treating modes without emitter linkage obscures which trains belong together after deinterleaving errors or overlap \parencite{wiley2006elint}.
The remainder of this chapter situates passive reception in the EW/ESM processing chain (\cref{sec:bg:elint_pipeline}) and then supplies the learning background needed to formalize such joint structure in the method.
