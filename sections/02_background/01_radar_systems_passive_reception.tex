\section{Radar Systems and Passive Reception}\label{sec:bg:radar}

\subsection{Pulses for navigation, surveillance, and control}

Engineered \gls{radar} grew from wartime range finding into a family of microwave systems used for air traffic control, weather mapping, altimetry, ship and vehicle navigation, and target tracking \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
The common thread is practical: launch a controlled electromagnetic disturbance, let it scatter, and read geometry and motion from the returned field.
At the level of Maxwell's equations, radiation follows accelerating charge and current patterns in an antenna; for the purposes here it suffices to treat each emitted burst as a localized wave packet whose group delay encodes distance once propagation speed is known \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.

\subsection{Echo delay, ambiguity, and biosonar}

Monostatic ranging ties round-trip delay $\tau$ to range $R$ by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:range}
R = \frac{c\tau}{2},
\end{equation}
with $c$ the propagation speed \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
The same bookkeeping appears in animal echolocation: a bat emits a short click, listens, and treats the echo arrival time as evidence about reflector distance, only with sound instead of radio \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
Trains of pulses repeat with \gls{pri} or \gls{prf}. A late echo from one pulse can be mistaken for an early echo from the next, which caps the unambiguous range by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:runamb}
R_{\mathrm{u}} = \frac{c}{2\,\mathrm{PRF}} = \frac{c\,\mathrm{PRI}}{2}.
\end{equation}
Coherent matched filtering and integration still raise \gls{snr} at the detector, but the ambiguity identity in \cref{eq:bg:runamb} is the structural constraint that survives independent of hardware details \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.

\subsection{Transmitting radars versus receive-only systems}

A pulsed radar platform both illuminates and listens, so it can exploit a known transmit waveform when forming correlations and Doppler filters \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
Passive \gls{esm} and \gls{elint} systems instead sit on the sidelobe or distant mainlobe energy of someone else's transmitter.
They do not control $P_t$, antenna gain schedules, or internal timing laws, so cooperative echo models are largely unavailable.
Inference therefore leans on measured per-pulse parameters (carrier, arrival time, width, angle, amplitude) rather than on matched filtering against a secret template.
That posture is exactly what motivates the \gls{pdw} abstraction developed below and detailed further in \cref{sec:bg:elint_pipeline}.

\subsection{From passband samples to a list of pulses}

A typical front end produces a digitized passband or near-baseband record $x[n]$ at a sample rate high enough to capture the emitted bandwidth.
Energy detection marks candidate pulse intervals against thermal noise, after which parameter estimation attaches scalars to each event.
Spectral location of narrowband energy is naturally read from a windowed discrete Fourier transform,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bg:stft}
X_m[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} w[n]\, x[n + m H]\, e^{-i 2\pi kn/N},
\end{equation}
with window $w[n]$, hop $H$, and bin index $k$ tied to physical frequency through the tuning plan \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
Time of arrival and pulse width are then obtained from correlation against a template or from threshold crossings on the envelope, depending on the receiver \parencite{wiley2006elint}.
The intermediate waveform is discarded once those numbers are stable, which is why later learning stages never see the raw $x[n]$.

\subsection{Pulse descriptor words and the pulse train}\label{sec:bg:pdw}

After detection and parameter estimation in the pipeline of \cref{sec:bg:elint_pipeline}, each accepted pulse is summarized by a \emph{\gls{pdw}}: a fixed-layout record of measured pulse parameters that discards intermediate waveforms while retaining the quantities downstream algorithms consume \parencite{wiley2006elint}.
Index pulses in time order by $n \in \{1,2,\ldots\}$ and write the $n$th descriptor as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bg:pdw_vector}
  \vect{x}_n
  =
  \bigl(
    f_n,\,
    \mathrm{PW}_n,\,
    t_n,\,
    A_n,\,
    \theta_n,\,
    \ldots
  \bigr)^{\top}
  \in \R^{d},
\end{equation}
where $f_n$ is the measured carrier frequency, $\mathrm{PW}_n$ the pulse width, $t_n$ the \gls{toa} of a chosen reference point (often the leading edge), $A_n$ a scalar amplitude or energy proxy, and $\theta_n$ the \gls{aoa} when direction finding exists.
The ellipsis stands for any extra columns the sensor exports (sub-band tags, polarization flags, intra-pulse modulation labels); $d$ is fixed once the column order is frozen.
\cref{fig:bg:pdw} matches the minimal five-field template implied by \cref{eq:bg:pdw_vector}.

For a single coherent pulse train, successive \glspl{toa} define inter-pulse intervals
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bg:pdw_pri}
  \tau_n = t_n - t_{n-1}, \qquad n \ge 2,
\end{equation}
so a nominal \gls{pri} is read from $\tau_n$ and a \gls{prf} follows as $\mathrm{PRF}_n = 1/\tau_n$ when the train is close to periodic.
Agile \gls{pri} schedules replace one constant $\tau_n$ with structured sequences; either $\tau_n$ is stored per pulse or extra columns carry stagger metadata, but each index $n$ still labels one row in $\R^{d}$ as in \cref{eq:bg:pdw_vector}.

Measured descriptors obey $\vect{x}_n = \vect{g}(\vect{s}_n) + \vect{\epsilon}_n$ with latent physical state $\vect{s}_n$, deterministic sensor map $\vect{g}$, and aggregate error $\vect{\epsilon}_n$ from noise, quantization, and estimator bias.
Weak pulses inflate effective noise or leave fields blank; false alarms inject samples with no meaningful $\vect{s}_n$.
\emph{Deinterleaving} (\cref{sec:bg:elint_pipeline}) partitions detections into trains so that \cref{eq:bg:pdw_pri} is interpretable inside each train; mixture, misses, and spurious hits then show up as structured outliers in the sequence $(\vect{x}_n)$.

For the learning part of this thesis, the model input is a \emph{time-ordered \gls{pdw} sequence} $(\vect{x}_1,\ldots,\vect{x}_T)$, that is, a \emph{pulse train} (possibly after association and sorting) rather than the raw digitized waveform.
Each $\vect{x}_n$ is one token in a length-$T$ sequence whose coordinates carry explicit physical units and semantics, which matters when designing encoders and augmentation.

\subsection{Physical sources and transmit schedules}\label{sec:bg:emitters}

In passive \gls{esm} and \gls{elint}, a \emph{radar emitter} means a physical transmitter whose pulses line up in time and frequency strongly enough to be treated as one source \parencite{wiley2006elint}.
An \emph{operating mode} is the instantaneous transmit recipe: carrier plan, pulse width, repetition pattern, beam motion, and any programmed switching rules among them \parencite{skolnik2008radar,wiley2006elint}.
The same hardware cycles among modes as tasks change, so a timeline of \glspl{pdw} mixes mode segments even when the physical box is fixed.
Different platforms can also implement the same functional mode with different numerical parameters, for example two trackers with distinct nominal \glspl{prf} \parencite{wiley2006elint}.

Illustrative mode families include wide-area search, acquisition and track, fire-control illumination, weather sensing, and terminal guidance support \parencite{skolnik2008radar}.
Those roles push different combinations of \gls{pri}, dwell, agility, and scan law, which is why operators care both about identity and about what the transmitter is doing at a given time \parencite{skolnik2008radar,wiley2006elint}.

The relationship between emitters and modes is many-to-many: one emitter runs many modes over a mission, and different emitters can realize similar modes with different parameter sets \parencite{wiley2006elint}.
The rest of this chapter walks through the passive processing chain (\cref{sec:bg:elint_pipeline}) and then supplies the learning background used in the method.
